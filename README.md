# DV495_EFLR_Agency
# Dissertation: Women's Agency as a Conversion Factor in Economic Development  This repository contains all code and documentation for my MSc dissertation investigating whether increases in women's agency shape the extent to which local economic opportunities reduce multidimensional poverty in Ethiopia.  

# Dissertation Proposal (Updated)

## Title (working)
**Women’s Agency as a Conversion Factor: Legal Reform, Economic Activity, and Material Wellbeing in Ethiopia**

---

## 1. Motivation and Research Question

This dissertation investigates whether women’s agency shapes the extent to which economic opportunities translate into improvements in material wellbeing. This draws on Sen’s capability approach, which argues that expanding resources is not enough to improve wellbeing unless individuals have the agency required to convert those resources into valued achievements.

**Research question:**  
*Does women’s agency act as a “conversion factor” that enhances the poverty-reducing effect of local economic activity?*

Establishing this mechanism is empirically difficult because agency and poverty may influence each other and share common unobservable determinants. To overcome this, I use an external shock to women’s agency generated by a major legal reform.

---

## 2. Policy Context and Exogenous Variation

The empirical strategy exploits the rollout of Ethiopia’s Revised Family Code (RFC), a reform that significantly strengthened women’s legal rights. Key changes included:

- Women gained authority to administer marital property.  
- Spouses could no longer block the other from working outside the home.  
- Courts were granted greater authority over divorce and inheritance disputes.  
- The minimum legal marriage age was increased to 18.  

### Rollout pattern
- In the year 2000, **no region** had adopted the RFC.  
- Between **2000 and 2005**, **five regions** implemented the reform.  
- By **2005**, **all regions** had adopted it.  
- After 2005, there is **no further cross-regional variation** in treatment.

Because adoption timing was driven by administrative capacity rather than economic trends, early- and late-adopting regions are plausibly comparable prior to the reform. All identifying treatment variation therefore comes from the **single differential reform window between 2000 and 2005**.

---

## 3. Data

I use data from Ethiopia’s Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS):

- **2000 DHS** (pre-reform period)  
- **2005 DHS** (post-reform period with variation across regions)  

These are the only waves with credible pre-treatment and differential post-treatment exposure.  

I additionally use **2011** and **2016** DHS waves **only for the IV mechanism** (because after 2005 all regions are treated, but agency and economic activity continue to vary).

Night‑time lights are merged to DHS clusters (PSUs) using PSU-year identifiers.

---

## 4. Key Variables

### 4.1 Women’s agency deprivation (individual level)
An agency deprivation index constructed using an Alkire–Foster style methodology based on:

- decision-making autonomy,  
- attitudes toward domestic violence,  
- and bodily autonomy indicators.

Higher values = *lower agency*.

### 4.2 Material standard of living (SoL)
The material standard of living dimension of the multidimensional poverty index (MPI).  
This is chosen because the education and health dimensions may be directly affected by the RFC, which would violate the exclusion restriction.

### 4.3 Local economic activity (PSU level)
Night‑time lights (NTL), merged at the PSU-year level, used as a proxy for local economic development.

### 4.4 Treatment / instrument (region level)
A binary treatment variable defined as:

- `Treated_region`: 1 if a region adopted RFC by 2005, 0 otherwise.  
- `Post_2005`: 1 in 2005, 0 in 2000.  
- Instrument: `Treated_region * Post_2005`.

This is the *only* period with treatment variation.

---

## 5. Empirical Strategy

### 5.1 First Stage: Effect of RFC on Agency Deprivation (2×2 DiD)

Because all cross-regional variation occurs between 2000 and 2005, I estimate:

**Agency Deprivation =**  
`β0 + β1*Treated_region + β2*Post_2005 + β3*(Treated_region × Post_2005) + controls + error`

- The coefficient β3 measures the causal effect of the reform on agency deprivation.  
- A negative β3 indicates that the RFC reduced deprivation (i.e., increased agency).

**Inference:**  
Treatment is assigned at the **region** level, so standard errors are clustered at the region level.  
Because only five regions adopted early, I will also use **wild cluster bootstrap** or **randomization inference** to obtain reliable p-values.

---

### 5.2 Second Stage: Agency as a Conversion Factor (IV Mechanism)

To test whether agency strengthens the effect of economic activity on SoL, I estimate:

**Second Stage (outcome):**  
`SoL = θ1*NTL + θ2*Agency + θ3*(Agency × NTL) + controls + error`

The key parameter is θ3:  
*Does economic activity improve material wellbeing more when women have higher agency?*

### Instrumentation
Because agency is endogenous and the key term is an interaction, I instrument:

- Endogenous regressor: `(Agency × NTL)`  
- Instrument: `(Treated_region × Post_2005) × NTL`

This uses reform-induced changes in agency interacted with exogenous economic conditions.

**Inference:**  
All IV models are clustered at the **region** level because that is the level at which the policy varies.

## 6. Contribution

This dissertation contributes to three literatures:

1. **Gender and empowerment:**  
   By estimating the causal effect of a major family law reform on women’s agency.

2. **Poverty reduction mechanisms:**  
   By testing whether women’s agency determines how effectively economic activity reduces deprivation.

3. **Applied microeconometrics with multi-level data:**  
   By combining a natural experiment (RFC rollout) with an IV-interaction model and PSU-level night‑time lights in repeated cross-sections.
